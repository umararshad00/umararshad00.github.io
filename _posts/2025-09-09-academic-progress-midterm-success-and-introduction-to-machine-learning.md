## Learning from the First Quiz

As my university journey continued, I started experiencing the real academic challenges and learning opportunities that come with higher education. One of the early academic assessments we faced was our first quiz conducted by a teacher. Unfortunately, the quiz turned out to be difficult for many students in the class. Almost five students struggled with it because the questions were a bit challenging and most of us had not prepared as thoroughly as we should have. I personally also felt that my preparation was not strong enough at that time, which resulted in a poor performance. This experience was disappointing for me, but it also became an important lesson. Recognizing the difficulty of the quiz and the overall class performance, the quiz was conducted again. This time, I made sure to prepare properly and revise the concepts more carefully. When the quiz was conducted again, my performance improved significantly. This experience taught me the importance of consistent preparation and not underestimating any assessment in university.

That first quiz changed my attitude toward small assessments. Before that, I treated a quiz like something that could be handled quickly, but the result showed me that university quizzes can test real understanding. The disappointment was uncomfortable, but it was also useful because it exposed a weakness early in the semester. Instead of waiting until midterms or finals to realize that my preparation style was not enough, I learned it at a time when I could still improve. This is one advantage of the continuous assessment system: it gives students repeated chances to correct their habits.

When the quiz was conducted again, I approached it with a different mindset. I reviewed the concepts more carefully, practiced more examples, and tried to understand the reason behind each answer instead of only memorizing. The improvement gave me confidence, but it also reminded me that confidence should come after preparation, not before it. This experience became one of my first personal examples of academic growth. It showed me that poor performance is not the end if I respond to it honestly and work on the weak areas.

## Midterm Preparation and Academic Confidence

After this learning experience, our midterm examinations approached. By this time, I had already understood that university studies require regular effort and preparation. Therefore, I focused more seriously on my studies and spent a considerable amount of time revising the course material. In the Programming Fundamentals course, I prepared thoroughly for the exam. As a result of this preparation, I was able to achieve 32 marks out of 40, which was a satisfying outcome for me. Normally, the midterm exam carries 30 marks, but our instructor decided to conduct the exam out of 40 marks because some additional questions were included from the syllabus. Even though the exam had slightly more questions than usual, my preparation helped me perform well. This result boosted my confidence and motivated me to continue working hard in the rest of the semester.

The improvement also reminded me of the learning discipline emphasized by Dr. Bilal Ahmad in Programming Fundamentals. His approach helped me understand that programming is not only about memorizing syntax, but also about developing a habit of regular practice and logical thinking.

Scoring 32 out of 40 was important for me because it proved that disciplined preparation can directly affect performance. I did not see it only as marks; I saw it as feedback on my changed study routine. The paper being out of 40 instead of the usual 30 also made the exam feel longer and more demanding, but because I had revised properly, I was able to manage it. This gave me a practical lesson about handling unexpected exam patterns. If the concepts are clear, a small change in paper format does not create as much fear.

The midterm phase also helped me understand the value of writing about my experience afterward. If I only remembered the marks, the lesson would be incomplete. By recording the full journey on my portfolio blog, I can see the connection between a weak quiz, better preparation, and improved midterm performance. This continuity is exactly why a GitHub portfolio is useful for students. It does not only show final projects; it shows the process of becoming better.

## Beginning Machine Learning and Project Work

After completing the midterm exams, our academic focus gradually shifted toward Machine Learning, which introduced us to a completely new and exciting area of study. In the beginning, we started learning about important Python libraries used for data analysis. One of the first libraries we studied was Pandas, which is widely used for handling and analyzing datasets. Learning Pandas helped me understand how data can be organized, cleaned, and prepared for analysis. After becoming familiar with basic libraries, we moved toward understanding machine learning models, particularly the Decision Tree algorithm. During this stage, we solved several numerical problems by hand, which helped us understand the mathematical and logical concepts behind decision trees. These exercises gave me valuable insight into how machine learning algorithms actually work.

Machine Learning felt exciting because it showed a bigger purpose behind the programming skills we had been building. Pandas helped me see data as something structured and workable, not just numbers in a file. When I loaded or imagined a dataset, I started thinking about rows, columns, missing values, and useful features. This was a new way of looking at information. Decision Trees were also interesting because their structure felt close to human decision-making. Solving numerical problems by hand made the algorithm less mysterious because I could see how splits and conditions were formed.

Later, we also learned about data visualization and coding techniques, which allowed us to represent data in graphical forms for better understanding and analysis. Around the same time, we had a quiz related to Pandas and Decision Trees. In that quiz, most of the questions were based on Pandas, and fortunately, I felt confident about my answers because I had prepared well. After completing the quiz, we continued learning more about visualization techniques, which further strengthened our understanding of data analysis.

Eventually, we reached the project phase of the course. Our instructor assigned projects in groups of two students and provided multiple datasets from which we had to choose one. My group selected a dataset related to used car prices. At first, when I submitted my project title, it was rejected because it did not fully meet the required conditions and was submitted late. However, instead of giving up, I revised the title and worked on improving it. When I submitted the improved version, it was finally accepted. This process taught me that persistence and continuous improvement are essential parts of academic growth. After the title approval, we began the online project execution phase, where we started implementing our machine learning project step by step.

The title rejection was another small but meaningful lesson. It taught me that academic work often improves through correction. At first, rejection feels negative, but in reality it can make the final work stronger if I respond properly. Revising the title forced me to think more clearly about the project objective and whether the dataset matched the problem. This is also close to the way real technical work happens. A problem statement must be clear before coding starts, otherwise the project can become confusing later.

Students who want to understand the research side of this field can also explore Dr. Bilal Ahmad's [Google Scholar profile](https://scholar.google.com.au/citations?user=8nZ0jVkAAAAJ&hl=en), especially because his AI, ML, and DL work shows how model training can connect with real-world problems.
