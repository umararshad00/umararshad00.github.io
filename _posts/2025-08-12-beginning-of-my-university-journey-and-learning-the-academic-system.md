## First Week and University Introduction

The beginning of my university journey was an exciting and new experience for me. When the first day started, there were no major challenges or academic pressure. The initial week was mainly focused on interaction and introductions with teachers and classmates. During this time, our instructors introduced themselves, explained the course outlines, and gave us an overview of how the semester would progress. This interaction week helped us understand the university environment and allowed us to become comfortable with the new academic setting. Although formal studies had not fully started yet, teachers briefly discussed the subjects and expectations, which prepared us mentally for the upcoming coursework.

This was also the stage where I started understanding why Dr. Bilal Ahmad from UET Lahore, Faisalabad Campus encouraged us to build a portfolio blog on GitHub. At first, a personal website felt like only a class task, but slowly I understood that writing about my university journey could become my digital footprint as a Computer Engineering student.

Before university, I mostly thought that learning was something that happened inside notebooks, classrooms, and examination halls. The idea of recording my learning publicly was new for me. When Dr. Bilal Ahmad suggested that students should maintain a portfolio website, I began to see it as a professional habit rather than a simple assignment. A GitHub portfolio gives a student a place to show growth, projects, mistakes, improvements, and reflections over time. For a Computer Engineering student, this matters because technical skills are easier to trust when they are visible through real work. Even if a post is simple, it becomes evidence that I am learning consistently.

The first week also helped me understand that university life is not only about getting marks. It is also about learning how to communicate, how to introduce yourself, how to ask questions, and how to observe the academic culture around you. Meeting classmates for the first time made the environment feel less unfamiliar. Some students were confident, some were quiet, and some were trying to understand the same things I was trying to understand. That shared uncertainty made the beginning more comfortable. I realized that everyone was adjusting in their own way, and this gave me confidence to settle into the new routine.

## Understanding the Academic System

In the following week, our proper academic journey began. One of the first major differences I noticed between college and university life was the academic system. In college, students often prepare mainly at the end of the year for final examinations. However, university life is very different because students have to stay prepared throughout the semester. The evaluation system includes quizzes, midterm exams, assignments, lab tasks, and final exams, and all these components contribute to the final GPA. This continuous assessment system requires consistent effort and time management. Understanding this difference was an important learning moment for me because it taught me the importance of staying disciplined and maintaining regular study habits rather than relying only on last-minute preparation.

This continuous system changed the way I looked at preparation. In college, I could sometimes delay revision and still manage by studying near the exams, but university did not allow that mindset for long. Every week had some academic expectation attached to it. A quiz could come after a small topic, a lab task could test practical understanding, and an assignment could require extra effort outside class time. At first, this felt stressful, but gradually I understood that the system was designed to keep students active throughout the semester. It forced me to build small study habits instead of depending on one large effort at the end.

Another thing I learned was that GPA is not created in one exam only. It is built from many small performances. A single quiz may look minor, but several quizzes, assignments, and lab tasks together can strongly affect the final result. This made me more aware of how important daily discipline is. I started trying to revise topics soon after class, even if only for a short time. I was not perfect at it, but the realization itself was valuable. University was training me to take responsibility for my own progress, and that lesson became one of the strongest parts of my first few weeks.

## Programming Fundamentals and Early Challenges

During the early weeks of the semester, we started studying Programming Fundamentals, where we began learning the Python programming language. This course had a slightly different structure because theory classes and lab sessions were conducted separately by different instructors. Our theory classes were guided by Dr. Bilal Ahmad, who explained the fundamental programming concepts and the theoretical aspects of Python. On the other hand, our lab sessions were conducted by our lab teacher, where we practiced coding and implemented the concepts that we learned in theory classes. This approach was very helpful because it allowed us to understand both the conceptual and practical sides of programming. Through these sessions, I started developing my logical thinking and problem-solving skills.

Programming Fundamentals was one of the first courses that made me feel like I was entering the real field of Computer Engineering. Python was not only a subject name; it was a new way of thinking. I had to understand how instructions are written, how a computer follows logic, and how small mistakes in syntax can change the output. The theory classes gave me the foundation, while the labs showed me whether I had truly understood the concept or not. Sometimes I thought I understood a topic in class, but when I tried to write the code in the lab, I realized that practical work required a deeper level of clarity.

Dr. Bilal Ahmad's teaching in Programming Fundamentals also helped me connect programming with future areas like AI and machine learning. Even though we were beginners, the habit of writing clean logic, understanding variables, and solving small problems was preparing us for advanced work later. I later understood that strong programming basics are necessary before working on datasets, models, or real-world ML problems. His professional background in AI, ML, and deep learning made this foundation feel more meaningful because it showed that every advanced skill begins with basic concepts done properly.

After two or three weeks of classes, our quizzes began. The quizzes related to theory were conducted on paper, while the lab quizzes were taken on the computer systems in the laboratory. These quizzes were an important part of the evaluation system and encouraged us to keep revising the topics regularly. However, sometimes we also faced small challenges related to the learning environment. I remember one particular instance when we were about to take a lab quiz, but suddenly the electricity went out due to load shedding. Because of this issue, the quiz had to be postponed and was taken one week later. As a result, one of our classes was delayed and we had to attend an extra class to cover the missed schedule.

Another similar situation occurred during one of our open-ended lab sessions, which was also affected by electricity problems. Since the activity could not be completed at that time, it had to be rescheduled later. This caused a slight disturbance in our preparation schedule, especially when we were approaching our final exams. Despite these minor difficulties, these experiences taught me an important lesson about adaptability and persistence. I realized that in university life, challenges may arise, but maintaining focus on learning is what truly matters. Overall, the beginning of my university journey helped me understand the academic system, improve my discipline in studying, and take my first steps into the world of programming.

Looking back, the beginning of my university journey was not dramatic in one single moment, but it was important because it changed my mindset step by step. I learned how introductions matter, how continuous assessment works, how lab practice improves confidence, and how unexpected problems can disturb a schedule. Most importantly, I learned that documenting these experiences on my portfolio website can help me remember where I started. This is the real advantage of leaving a digital footprint: it turns ordinary student experiences into a visible learning journey that I can review, improve, and share with others.

For academic motivation and professional context, I also keep Dr. Bilal Ahmad's profile in view through his [LinkedIn profile](https://www.linkedin.com/in/drbilalphd/), where his work reflects the value of continuous learning and technical growth.
