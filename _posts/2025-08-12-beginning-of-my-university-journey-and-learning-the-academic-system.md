The beginning of my university journey was an exciting and unfamiliar experience. On the first day, there was no major academic pressure, and the environment felt welcoming rather than overwhelming. The initial week was mostly focused on interaction with teachers and classmates, which helped all of us become comfortable with the new setting.

During this introduction period, our instructors explained the course outlines, introduced themselves, and shared how the semester would progress. Even though formal studies had not fully started yet, these early conversations were important because they mentally prepared us for university expectations. I started understanding that university life would be more structured, disciplined, and independent than what I had experienced before.

When the following week began, our proper academic journey started. One of the first major differences I noticed between college and university was the evaluation system. In college, students often focus mainly on end-of-year examinations, but university requires steady preparation throughout the semester. Quizzes, assignments, lab tasks, midterms, and final exams all contribute to the final GPA.

This continuous assessment system immediately changed my mindset. I realized that success in university is not based on last-minute preparation. Instead, it depends on consistency, time management, and regular revision. That early realization became one of the most valuable lessons of my first semester.

Around the same time, we also started studying Programming Fundamentals and were introduced to Python. This course had an interesting structure because theory and lab sessions were conducted separately. Our theory classes were taught by Sir Bilal, who explained the basic concepts and logic of programming, while our lab sessions were conducted by Miss Rimsha, where we practiced the same ideas through coding.

This theory-and-lab combination helped me understand both the conceptual and practical sides of programming. I was not only learning syntax, but also beginning to develop logical thinking and a problem-solving mindset. Seeing concepts in theory and then implementing them in the lab made the learning process much more effective.

After two or three weeks, our quizzes began. Theory quizzes were conducted on paper, while lab quizzes were taken on computer systems in the laboratory. These small assessments encouraged us to keep revising regularly. I also remember one lab quiz being postponed because of load shedding, which delayed our schedule and forced us to attend an extra class later.

Although these early weeks included small disruptions, they taught me something important: adaptability matters just as much as preparation. Overall, the beginning of my university journey helped me understand the academic system, improve my study discipline, and take my first real steps into the world of programming.
